U.S. and Soviet Union Philosophies
National Archives at College Park, Public domain, via Wikimedia Commons

The United States and Soviet Union had very different philosophies about how government and society should work. These differences were one of the main focuses of the war and the reasoning between many of the proxy battles.

The United States

The United States' philosophy and attitude towards the Cold War was that it was a necessary response to the communist agression from the Soviet Union. They felt that they needed to combat communism's influence and spread. Instead, the United States felt that capitalism was the superior economic ideology. Under capitalism, the economy is mostly free from the government's control. In March 1947, the current U.S. president, President Truman, issued what would become known as the Truman Doctrine. He announced a policy to contain communism and prevent it from spreading. He then accused the Soviets of encouraging dicatorships in other nations. The United States would go on to be a part of NATO, with a mission to stop communist expansion in Europe.

The Soviet Union

The Soviet Union, on the other hand, supported communism as the "correct" economic ideology. In October of 1917, the Bolshevik Revolution turned Russia into a communist state. Communist states have centralized administration of the economy. In addition, communist states give the government control of the society. Stalin wanted a buffer zone between his country and the Western countries like Great Britain. In order to accomplish this, he established communist regimes in neighboring eastern European countries. This action is what gained the opposition of the United States, who wanted to create democratic states instead. In reaction to part of Germany joining NATO, Stalin and co. established the Warsaw Pact to counter it.
