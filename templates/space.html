Space Race

{% endcall %} {{single_img( 'https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/Space_Race_1957-1975.jpg', 'Tdadamemd, CC BY-SA 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons' )}}

The United States and Soviet Union didn't just seek military superiority. The two nations would try to be superior to the other in as many ways as possible. A new area of exploration, space, was a critical part of the Cold War. Demonstrating superiority through feats of aeronautics improves morale of soldiers and citizens. Additionally, launching objects into space, including satellites, had a strategic advantage. Satellites could spy on adversaries without recourse, and rockets could launch missiles at high speeds.

{{sbs_img( 'https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Sputnik_1.jpg', 'U.S. Air Force photo, Public domain, via Wikimedia Commons' )}}

The start of space exploration from the two powers began in the years after World War II. This occured when both nations realized that the rockets that they were developing for defense could be used in space exploration as well.

The major successes in the space race at first were completing a certain task first. These included the first satellite, first human orbiting the Earth, first human spending a day in Space, and impacting the Moon with an object. Nearly all of these "firsts" were accomplished by the Soviet Union. The first satellite, Sputnik, was launched by the Soviets in October 1957. The Soviets also got the first man and woman in space, as well as the first spacewalk. In an attempt to beat the Soviets at something space-related, President Kennedy set his sights on landing a human on the moon. Through programs such as the Mercury and Gemini missions, the United States built equipment suitable for space travel. On July 21, 1969, Apollo 11 touched down on the Moon, and the astronauts stepped out.
