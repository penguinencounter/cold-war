Afghanistan is a strategic asset, because it provides an advantageous position in Central Asia. Great Britain pre-USSR Russia battled for it for many years prior to the Cold War. After Afghanistan became independent of Great Britain, the Soviet Union established relations with the Afghan government. As Great Britain and the United States spread into Asia, the USSR felt threatened by their efforts in the neighboring country of Pakistan. On December 26, 1979, Soviet Union tanks invaded Afghanistan in order to stop a new rebel group. They also wanted to enforce the ideals of the Brezhnev Doctrine, which stated that once a nation became socialist, the USSR would prevent it from becoming capitalist again. These rebels, known as the mujahideen, rejected the current communist government and demanded a new one. The Soviets believed that this would be an easy battle, because the mujahideen were not very well armed. The battle with the Soviets would continue for ten years.

{{sbs_img( 'https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Road_to_Jalalabad.jpg', 'Todd Huffman from Phoenix, AZ, CC BY 2.0, via Wikimedia Commons' )}}

When the Soviets invaded, they did not get the battle that they were expecting. Due to the difficult terrain, large weapons like tanks were not suitable for warfare in Afghanistan. Additionally, the mujahideen, which had lived in Afghanistan for a long time, were familiar with how to effectively fight. The rebels also employed guerilla warfare tactics and stealth to attack the Soviets. The United States, under President Carter, supplied economic aid to the mujahideen to prevent the attack.
