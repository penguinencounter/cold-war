Cuba

After the revolution in Cuba led by Fidel Castro, Cuba's new government cut ties with the United States. Instead, Castro started working with the Soviet Union instead. These actions made the United States very concerned due to how close Cuba is to the United States. In March 1960, the President, Dwight D. Eisenhower, directed the CIA to prepare to invade Cuba and overthrow Castro. After John F. Kennedy was elected, after consulting the CIA plans, he concluded that Fidel Castro was effectively a member of the Soviet Union, and approved the plan. However, this attack, known as the Bay of Pigs invasion, went horribly wrong and all the troops were defeated by Castro in two days. Additionally, this attack spurred Castro to develop stronger ties with the USSR, and publicly announced the adoption of socialism for Cuba.

After the failure of the Bay of Pigs invasion, a second plan, named Operation Mongoose, was formed with a similar goal. However, the method of removing Castro was different. Instead of going directly for the government, the United States would try to destabilize the government in a more covert way, including anti-socialist propaganda and sabotage. However, this plan failed to remove Castro as well, and the Soviet Union had started to ship weapons to Cuba.

The Missile Crisis

The United States was concerned that the aforementioned weapons being shipped to Cuba were in fact missiles with nuclear warheads. In secret, Fidel Castro of Cuba and Nikita Khrushchev of the Soviet Union made an agreement to put Soviet nuclear missiles on Cuba's land in order to deter invasions. While attempts were made to build up the sites discreetly, they were noticed by United States intelligence from the air. On October 14, the United States had clear pictures of nuclear missiles being constructed in Cuba, and the next day, the White House had been alerted of the threat. On October 22, the President decided to quarantine (blockade but not go to war with) Cuba. The President also sent a message to Khrushchev that weapons would not be allowed in Cuba and demanded the removal of the missile launchers. At this point, launching the missiles would cause a thermonuclear war. Eventually, Khrushchev and Kennedy agreed on a plan: to remove the Soviet missiles from Cuba in exchange for removing the US missiles from Turkey.
